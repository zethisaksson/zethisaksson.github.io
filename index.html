About

Why do people vote the way they do?

Most of my work comes back to that question, and to a hunch that goes with it: the patterns we see in politics today often mirror ones from the past. I am a Max Weber Fellow at the European University Institute, working in comparative politics, historical political economy, political behaviour, and quantitative methods.

Empirically I work mostly with Swedish and post-socialist administrative and survey data, historical records, and quasi-experimental designs. My research has appeared in Electoral Studies, Party Politics, Acta Politica, the Journal of Elections, Public Opinion & Parties, Energy Policy, and Sociologia Ruralis, among others.

I received my PhD from Stockholm University in 2025 and have held visiting positions at the EUI, the WZB in Berlin, and Stockholm University's Department of Political Science.

I'm always open to new collaborations. Much of my research is single-authored — I think taking a project from first question to finished paper on your own is part of becoming a complete researcher — but the questions I care about are usually better in good company.

Research

Two distinct profiles

My research runs along two distinct profiles. For the most part they stay separate — different questions, data, and literatures — but every so often they speak to one another.

Profile — Historical Political Economy

The long shadow of history

I use registry, census, and archival data to follow political behaviour across long stretches of time, asking what earlier regimes leave behind. Much of this work is set in post-socialist Europe, where I trace how party membership, protest, and repression under authoritarian rule continue to shape turnout and partisanship decades after transition.

Profile — The Radical Right

Structural and cultural roots of the far-right surge

Here I ask why support for the radical right has grown, and argue that neither economics nor culture explains it on its own. Working mostly in rural Sweden, I study how local shocks — school and business closures, alcohol access, infrastructure, candidate supply — combine with cultural attachments to move votes.
